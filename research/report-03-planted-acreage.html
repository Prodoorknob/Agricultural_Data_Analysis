1. What This Module Does

The Planted Acreage Prediction module forecasts how many acres of corn, soybeans, and wheat farmers will plant in the upcoming growing season — issued in February, six or more weeks before USDA's official Prospective Plantings report (released annually on March 31). The model uses pre-season economic signals: futures price ratios, input costs, prior-year yields, and crop rotation patterns.

Sample output: "2026 U.S. corn planted acreage forecast: 91.2 million acres (±4.1M) — soybean competition elevated due to current corn/soy price ratio of 2.31, slightly below the historical threshold favoring corn."

Why this module is a strong second build: It shares infrastructure almost entirely with Module 01 — USDA NASS QuickStats is already in the pipeline, CME futures data added for Module 02 is reused here, and the PostgreSQL schema extends cleanly. The model itself is simpler than yield prediction. Estimated build time after Phase 1 infra: 2–3 weeks.

The Market Timing Opportunity

USDA's Prospective Plantings report (March 31) and its predecessor Grain Stocks report move grain markets substantially when they surprise analysts. The value of this module is publishing a well-reasoned forecast in February — giving traders, cooperatives, and input suppliers 6+ weeks of advance visibility. Even an imperfect forecast published early is commercially valuable to users who need to make operational commitments before the USDA print.

The Core Signal: Corn-Soy Price Ratio

The single most predictive feature for corn vs. soybean acreage allocation is the corn-to-soybean price ratio computed from November harvest futures. When the ratio falls below approximately 2.2–2.3 (soybeans pay relatively more per acre), acreage shifts to soybeans. When above 2.5, corn wins. This ratio explains the majority of year-over-year acreage swings between the two crops. Fertilizer cost — particularly anhydrous ammonia prices — amplifies this effect: expensive nitrogen makes corn even less attractive when the price ratio is marginal.

4. Model Implementation

Recommended Architecture: Ridge Regression + XGBoost Ensemble

Planted acreage models operate on annual, state-level panels — a modest dataset by ML standards (50 states × ~3 crops × ~45 years = ~6,750 observations). A regularized linear model (Ridge) performs well and is highly interpretable; XGBoost adds value for capturing nonlinear interactions between price ratio and fertilizer cost regimes. Ensemble improves out-of-sample stability.

1
Annual Feature Construction (as of November 1 each year)
All features are computed from data available by November 1 — the natural model freeze date for a February forecast. Key features: Dec corn futures price, Nov soybean futures price, corn/soy price ratio, fertilizer cost index, prior-year yield (state × crop), prior-year planted acres (state × crop), 5-year yield trend (captures improving genetics), crop rotation index (fraction of corn-on-corn vs. corn-after-soy in state).
features: [corn_dec_futures, soy_nov_futures, corn_soy_ratio, anhydrous_price, prior_yield, prior_acres, yield_trend_5yr, rotation_index]
2
Training: State-Stratified Cross-Validation
Use leave-one-year-out cross-validation to simulate the forecasting scenario — train on all years except the target year, predict that year's acreage. This honestly reflects what the model would have known at each point in time. Train on 1980–2019, validate 2020–2022, test 2023–2024.
LeaveOneGroupOut(groups=year_index) # strict temporal hold-out
3
Crop Competition Constraint
Total cropland is approximately fixed at the state level in the short run — acres that switch to soybeans come from somewhere (primarily corn). Implement a soft constraint: the sum of corn + soybean + wheat acreage changes must roughly equal zero across the major field crop portfolio. This prevents physically implausible forecasts where all crops expand simultaneously.
constraint: Σ(predicted_Δacres_corn + predicted_Δacres_soy + predicted_Δacres_wheat) ≈ 0
4
National Rollup & Uncertainty Bands
Sum state-level forecasts to national totals. Uncertainty at the national level is narrower than state-level because state-level errors are partially offsetting. Report 80% prediction intervals. The UI should always display both state-level and national totals — the national total is the number markets watch; state breakdown is what supply chain planners need.
national_forecast = Σ state_forecasts; national_PI = propagate_uncertainty(state_CIs, correlation_matrix)
5
USDA Benchmark Comparison — Post-March 31
After the USDA Prospective Plantings report releases, automatically compute the forecast vs. actuals delta and store in an accuracy table. Surface this retrospective prominently in the UI — it builds credibility with users over successive seasons and provides honest accountability for model performance.
INSERT INTO acreage_accuracy(year, crop, model_forecast, usda_report, delta_pct)
Late-spring caveat: Wet spring conditions can force last-minute acreage switches after planting windows close — shifting corn acres to soybeans (soybeans have a later planting window). The model cannot anticipate spring weather at forecast time. Mitigate by flagging the NOAA spring precipitation outlook as a secondary advisory beside the February forecast.

6. Risks & Mitigations

High
Spring weather disruptions not captured. Excessively wet springs (2019 was the most notable example — 19.6M prevented planting acres) dramatically reduce corn acreage post-forecast. The model cannot predict spring weather in February. Mitigation: Add a Spring Disruption Risk advisory to the UI — pulling NOAA 90-day outlook for major corn belt states — flagged visually alongside the February forecast.
Medium
Policy shocks — crop insurance subsidies, ARC/PLC elections. Federal farm support programs shift planting incentives in ways not captured by price ratios alone. Mitigation: Add a binary feature for dominant crop in ARC-CO vs. PLC elections at the state level — this data is available from FSA annually.
Medium
Fertilizer price data latency. ERS publishes quarterly data with a 2–3 month lag. For the February forecast, the most recent available data is Q3 of the prior year. Mitigation: Use CME urea/anhydrous futures as a more current proxy for fertilizer cost; validate against lagged ERS actuals during training.
Low
State-level model for sparse wheat states. Wheat acreage in some plains states is highly variable with thin history. Mitigation: Cluster states by production profile (Corn Belt vs. Plains vs. South) and apply separate model weights per cluster rather than a single national model.
