Option 1
Crop Yield Prediction

What it does: Estimates how many bushels per acre a county will produce for corn, soybeans, or wheat — before harvest. The model looks at weather patterns (temperature, rainfall, drought stress), soil quality, and how crops are rated each week, then outputs a forecast like "Iowa corn expected at 192 bu/acre, down from last year's 198."

Why people care: Farmers can size grain storage and marketing contracts. Grain elevators can plan logistics. Analysts can flag counties likely to underperform USDA's benchmark before the official numbers come out.

Honest caveats: The model gets better as the season progresses. Early-season (April/May) forecasts are rough. By July/August, they're much more reliable. A simple county yield average often beats fancy ML when data is sparse — this isn't magic.

Option 2
Commodity Price Forecasting

What it does: Predicts corn, soybean, or wheat prices 1–6 months out. The model combines futures market signals, USDA supply/demand reports (WASDE), weather and drought trends, and macroeconomic indicators like the dollar exchange rate.

Why people care: Farmers deciding when to sell grain. Cooperatives locking in forward contracts. Lenders estimating farm revenue for the next crop year. The question "should I sell now or wait?" is one of the most asked in agriculture.

Honest caveats: Grain futures markets are highly efficient — meaning prices already reflect most available information. Our model won't reliably beat the futures curve. The real value is combining signals into a clean probability view ("60% chance corn stays above $4.20 through October") and surfacing when fundamentals look unusual. Frame it as decision support, not a crystal ball.

Option 3
Planted Acreage Prediction

What it does: Forecasts how many acres of corn vs. soybeans vs. wheat farmers will plant before the season starts — typically in early winter/spring. The model uses futures price ratios (is corn or soybeans paying better?), prior-year yields, input costs like fertilizer prices, and crop rotation patterns.

Why people care: USDA publishes its official "Prospective Plantings" report every late March, and markets move sharply when it surprises analysts. A model that posts a forecast in February before that report — even an imperfect one — has real value for traders, agronomists, and supply chain planners who need early visibility into next season's supply.

Honest caveats: Spring weather can shift planting decisions late (wet fields = delayed planting = acreage switches). The model's early-season estimates will be less reliable than those made closer to planting time.

Option 4
Drought & Yield Risk Scoring

What it does: Produces a weekly county-level risk score that combines current drought conditions, temperature outlooks, soil water-holding capacity, and how crops are currently rated. Output is a simple color-coded risk level — like a weather alert but for crop stress — updated every Thursday when the US Drought Monitor releases new data.

Why people care: This is the most immediately actionable output for a working farmer. It answers "should I be worried this week?" in plain terms, without needing to understand model internals. It also directly maps to USDA disaster declarations, FSA emergency programs, and crop insurance triggers, so it's highly policy-relevant.

Honest caveats: This is less a "prediction" and more a current-state risk composite. It tells you where stress is happening now and what seasonal outlooks suggest — not a precise yield forecast. Accuracy is hard to measure in traditional ML terms, but the underlying data sources (US Drought Monitor, NOAA outlooks) are government-grade and highly trusted.

Where the data comes from

All of these are free, government-run APIs — no licensing fees or scraping required.

USDA NASS QuickStats
Already in pipeline. Yields, prices, acreage, crop ratings.
NOAA Climate Data
Daily temperature & precipitation by station. Free API key.
NASA POWER
Solar radiation, humidity, VPD. No API key needed.
US Drought Monitor
Weekly drought severity by county. Free REST API, no auth.
SSURGO (NRCS)
Soil drainage class, water capacity by county. Static, one-time pull.
USDA WASDE
Monthly supply/demand report. Free CSV download.
CME Futures (Nasdaq DL)
Corn, soy, wheat daily prices. Free tier: 50 calls/day.
USDA ERS Costs
Annual cost of production per commodity. Free download.
