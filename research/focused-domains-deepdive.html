Domain 01 / Water Security & Quality
Predicting where water
runs out or turns toxic
Aquifer depletion and waterway contamination are slow-moving crises that almost always have detectable early signals — if someone is looking for them. That's the job.
46M
Americans on unmonitored private wells
40%
of US aquifers declining faster than recharge rate
Feasibility for Grad Scope
Data availability
USGS, NOAA, NASA — all free and well-documented
ML complexity
Time series + spatial features. XGBoost or LSTM both viable
Build time
8–12 wk
Including data wrangling and model validation
Real-world impact
Directly actionable for utilities & rural municipalities
Projects
Project A
Groundwater Depletion Forecasting
Predict aquifer water table levels 3–12 months ahead at the county level. The model combines historical USGS well depth measurements with NASA GRACE satellite gravity anomalies (which detect mass loss underground), NOAA precipitation, and drought indices.

Target users: Rural water utilities, state environmental agencies, farmers deciding whether to drill a new well.

Why it works: Groundwater depletion follows physical laws — drawdown is predictable from recharge rates, precipitation deficit, and extraction pressure. The USGS has 800,000+ monitoring wells with decades of readings.
Project B
Nutrient Runoff & River Contamination Windows
Predict 48–72 hr windows of elevated nitrate and phosphorus runoff into river systems by combining upstream NASS fertilizer application timing, NOAA rainfall forecasts, USGS stream gauge levels, and land cover (NLCD) to characterize how much agricultural surface drains to each monitoring point.

Target users: Downstream drinking water utilities, fisheries managers, recreational water safety officials.

Why it works: Runoff pulses are highly predictable — the first heavy rain after spring fertilizer application almost always spikes nitrate levels. Getting ahead of it by 48 hrs is the difference between a utility issuing a precautionary notice vs. a boil-water order.
Data Pipeline
From raw sensors to county-level forecasts
Ingest
USGS well depth API, NASA GRACE monthly files, NOAA CDO daily weather, EPA WQX water quality readings
Align
Spatially join well locations to county FIPS. Resample all data to monthly cadence. Impute sparse well records.
Engineer
Rolling precip anomaly, PDSI drought index, cumulative GDD, 3/6/12-month groundwater trend, aquifer stress ratio
Model
XGBoost with lag features OR LSTM on monthly sequences. SHAP for county-level explanation.
Surface
County choropleth risk map. 3-month rolling forecast. Threshold-triggered alerts by aquifer.

💡 Grad Project Angle

Project A (groundwater depletion) is the stronger single-semester project — the data is clean, the modeling is well-precedented, and the output (a county risk map with 3-month forecasts) is immediately legible to non-technical stakeholders. Project B is more technically interesting but requires careful spatial matching of watersheds to monitoring points, which adds scope. Either works as a standalone; combined, they'd make a strong capstone or publication.

Domain 02 / Food Access & Nutrition Security
Predicting where hunger
arrives before it's visible
Food banks run on donations that spike after a crisis is already in the news. A model that flags rising insecurity 6 months ahead lets them pre-position supplies, apply for emergency grants, and lobby before the line forms.
13.5%
US households food insecure in 2023 — highest in a decade
$2.4T
Annual economic cost of food insecurity in the US
Feasibility for Grad Scope
Data availability
Best-in-class public datasets. USDA FARA, Map the Meal Gap, Census ACS are all free and well-maintained.
ML complexity
Tabular regression + time series. Panel data across counties. Moderate complexity.
Build time
6–10 wk
Fastest to prototype of the four domains. Data is annual but rich.
Real-world impact
Food banks, SNAP administrators, and city governments are active consumers of this kind of analysis
Projects
Project A
Food Insecurity Early Warning System
Predict county-level food insecurity rates 6–12 months ahead by treating Feeding America's annual Map the Meal Gap county estimates as ground truth and building a lagged prediction model from economic, agricultural, and demographic leading indicators.

Key insight: Food insecurity is a lagged economic outcome — unemployment spikes show up in insecurity rates 3–9 months later. Commodity price increases for staples (wheat, corn, eggs) hit low-income households 2–4 months before they register in food bank demand data.

Target users: Food bank networks (Feeding America's 200 member banks), SNAP state agencies, community foundations allocating emergency grants.
Project B
Urban Food Desert Expansion Prediction
Predict which census tracts are on a 2-year trajectory toward losing adequate food access by modeling grocery store closures as a function of neighborhood demographic shifts (Census ACS income trends), commercial lease market pressure (building permit filings, business license data), and competitor proximity.

Key insight: Grocery store closures in low-income neighborhoods almost never happen suddenly — they follow a predictable decline: rising vacancy rates nearby, slipping foot traffic, demographic income decline, and a 12–18 month warning window in business permit filings before the closure.

Target users: City planners, USDA Rural Development grants program, community development financial institutions (CDFIs).
Data Pipeline
From economic signals to county food insecurity forecasts
Ingest
BLS unemployment claims (weekly), CPI food subcategories (monthly), SNAP enrollment (monthly), Census ACS (annual), USDA FARA (annual)
Align
Harmonize to county FIPS. Upsample annual census data using interpolation. Merge commodity price indices to household income quintiles.
Engineer
Unemployment shock index, food price burden ratio (CPI food / median income), SNAP enrollment rate change, grocery access score, housing cost pressure index
Model
Panel regression for baselines. XGBoost + lag features for prediction. Uncertainty bands via quantile regression.
Surface
County risk tier map. 6-month insecurity rate forecast with confidence interval. Top-5 driver explanation per county.

💡 Grad Project Angle

This is the most immediately publishable of the four. Map the Meal Gap is a well-known benchmark, which means your model has a clear evaluation target and a built-in comparison baseline. A county-level food insecurity forecasting model with SHAP explanations and a 6-month lead time would be genuinely novel — published work in this space focuses mostly on describing current insecurity, not predicting it. Strong fit for an applied data science capstone or journal submission. Also the easiest to pitch to Dr. Wanless or external collaborators because the social narrative is immediate and concrete.

Domain 04 / Housing & Displacement Risk
Predicting displacement
before families lose housing
Every eviction that reaches court has been building for months in the data — rent burden climbing, local unemployment ticking up, a missed utility payment. The signal is there. Most cities just aren't reading it.
3.6M
Eviction filings per year in the US
$35K
Avg. public cost per family entering shelter system after eviction
Feasibility for Grad Scope
Data availability
Eviction Lab data is gold standard. Census + BLS + HUD round out the feature set well.
ML complexity
Spatial panel model + classification. Harder than food/water due to data granularity gaps.
Build time
10–14 wk
Data joining across sources at tract level adds time. Ethical review recommended.
Real-world impact
Cities and housing nonprofits actively seek this kind of early warning infrastructure
Projects
Project A
Eviction Risk Scoring by Census Tract
Predict census tracts with elevated eviction filing probability in the next 6 months. Eviction Lab provides historical filing rates going back to 2000 across 50 states — this is the target variable. Features include rent burden (% income on rent), unemployment shock, SNAP enrollment change, utility shutoff rates, and local housing court backlog estimates.

Critical ethical note: The output should be framed as where to deploy resources, not which tenants are risky. The goal is directing legal aid, rental assistance, and mediation programs — not flagging individuals.

Target users: Housing stability programs, legal aid organizations, city housing departments, community development corporations.
Project B
Gentrification Trajectory Mapping
Predict which neighborhoods are 3–5 years from significant demographic displacement using a multi-signal model: building permit velocity, transit proximity changes (new stops announced), median rent appreciation rate, income in-migration from IRS migration data, and business type transition (laundromat → coffee shop as a signal of neighborhood repositioning).

Key insight: Gentrification has a detectable pre-displacement phase — rising permits and rent appreciation precede displacement by 2–4 years on average. Getting a signal that early gives communities time to push for inclusionary zoning, community land trusts, and tenant protections.

Target users: City planning departments, community land trust organizations, affordable housing advocates.
Data Pipeline
From housing market signals to tract-level displacement risk
Ingest
Eviction Lab filings, Census ACS rent/income, HUD CHAS housing affordability, BLS unemployment, IRS migration flows, local permit APIs
Align
Join all data to Census tract FIPS. Handle annual vs. monthly cadence mismatches. Geocode business license data.
Engineer
Rent burden index, rent appreciation velocity (3yr), permit density growth, income in-migration ratio, displacement pressure composite score
Model
Random Forest classifier for eviction risk tier. Gradient boosting regression for gentrification trajectory score. Spatial autocorrelation correction via Moran's I.
Surface
Tract-level risk choropleth. 6-month high-risk alert list. "Why this tract?" SHAP waterfall by neighborhood.

💡 Grad Project Angle

The Eviction Lab dataset is genuinely excellent and underused in predictive modeling — most published work describes eviction patterns retrospectively. A 6-month forward-looking risk model at the census tract level with ethical framing around resource allocation would be novel, publishable, and directly useful to housing nonprofits. The ethical dimension also makes this strong for interdisciplinary work or policy-facing presentations. One caution: scope to 1–3 cities for the tract-level work rather than attempting all 50 states — the permit and local data joining gets complex fast at national scale.

Domain 07 / Climate Migration & Displacement
Predicting where people
will flee — and where they'll land
Climate displacement isn't a future problem. It's happening now in slow motion — insurance markets withdrawing, property values softening, people quietly leaving. The communities that will receive them have almost no idea what's coming.
1.2M
Americans already displaced by climate-related disasters annually
13M
Projected US residents in climate-threatened coastal areas by 2100
Feasibility for Grad Scope
Data availability
FEMA, Census, NOAA, First Street Foundation all provide strong free data. Some gaps in insurance withdrawal signals.
ML complexity
Multi-horizon migration modeling is genuinely hard. Survival analysis + regression hybrid needed.
Build time
12–16 wk
Most complex of the four. Better suited for a full capstone or 2-semester project.
Real-world impact
Enormous long-term impact. Receiving cities desperately lack planning tools for this.
Projects
Project A
Wildfire Displacement Risk Forecasting
Predict which counties in fire-prone regions (Western US, increasingly Southeast) face significant population displacement risk over a 3–5 year horizon. The model combines USFS wildfire hazard potential maps, housing density in Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) zones, insurance market withdrawal signals (NFIP non-renewals, state FAIR plan enrollment spikes), and historical post-fire census migration patterns.

Key insight: Insurance withdrawal is an especially powerful early signal — when private insurers stop writing homeowner policies in a county, it precedes significant population decline by 3–5 years. California counties like El Dorado and Tuolumne show this pattern clearly.

Target users: State emergency management agencies, county planners in fire-prone regions, FEMA's hazard mitigation grant programs.
Project B
Receiving City Infrastructure Stress Prediction
Predict which mid-size inland cities (Boise, Spokane, Chattanooga, Knoxville, Bozeman, Indianapolis) will face housing, school, and infrastructure capacity stress from climate in-migration within 5–10 years. Combines historical migration flow patterns from IRS and Census data, climate exposure scores of the sending counties, housing affordability and capacity data of receiving metros, and employment market absorption capacity.

Key insight: Most people don't move far — climate migrants from coastal Florida overwhelmingly move to inland Florida, Georgia, and the Carolinas. Understanding these gravity-model migration patterns makes the receiving-city forecast tractable.

Target users: Receiving city planners, school districts, infrastructure investment programs, regional housing authorities.
Data Pipeline
From climate risk signals to migration pressure forecasts
Ingest
NOAA SLR projections, USFS wildfire hazard, FEMA NFIP claims/non-renewals, IRS migration flows, Census ACS, First Street Foundation flood/fire risk scores
Align
Build county-pair origin-destination matrix. Match climate exposure scores to sending counties. Map historic migration to climate event timelines.
Engineer
Insurance withdrawal rate, cumulative disaster declaration count, property value trajectory, flood/fire exposure score, housing affordability index at destination
Model
Gravity model baseline. XGBoost augmented with climate risk features. Survival analysis for time-to-displacement. Uncertainty cone for 5/10/20-year horizons.
Surface
Sending county displacement risk map. Receiving city pressure forecast (housing, schools, jobs). Origin-destination flow visualization.

💡 Grad Project Angle

This is the highest-ceiling of the four — but also the most complex. The wildfire displacement model (Project A) is the more tractable single-project scope: the data is clean, the insurance-withdrawal signal is underexplored in academic literature, and the Western US case studies are well-documented. Indianapolis is actually a strong receiving-city case study for Project B — it sits in a relatively low-risk climate corridor and has been experiencing in-migration from more climate-exposed metros. That local angle could make it especially relevant for the SII and IU context. Strongly recommend scoping to one sending region + one receiving corridor rather than attempting national coverage.
